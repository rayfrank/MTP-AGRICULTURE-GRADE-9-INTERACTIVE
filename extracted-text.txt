Conserving Animal Feed: Hay
During long and short rainy seasons, there is plenty of forage crops (pastures and foddercrops) for domestic animals in most parts of the country. However, during droughtseasons, there is scarcity of forage crops leading to starvation and death of the animals.
In pastoralist areas, farmers migrate their animals to faraway places in search of pasture.
This sometimes leads to communal conflicts as animals compete to graze on limitedpastures. Learning how to conserve excess forage can cushion households from foddershortages during dry seasons. We will learn how to conserve forage through making hay.
Methods of conserving forage
Forage are plants which grow naturally or are planted for feeding livestock animals.
The forage plants which are grazed directly by livestock are calledpastureswhile thosewhich are harvested and then fed to livestock are calledfodder crops
. We will focus onmethods of conserving forage as hay, namely baled hay, stacking hay and standing hay.

Forage crops for hay are harvested at the flowering stage because that is whenconcentration of nutrients is at the highest. The weather during the harvesting andpreparation stage should be hot and dry.
When harvesting manually, a sickle or scythe is used to cut the forage.
Harvesting forage for hay making
The cut forage is left to sun dry (wilt) for 2 to 3 days depending on the prevailingweather conditions. Within that period, the forage is spread evenly or turned using arake a few times to ensure uniform drying. The forage at that point should not breakeasily.

Baled hay is of higher quality in terms of nutritive value compared to stovers andstanding hay. The quality of hay is determined by factors such as:
a.
The type of forage which is harvested. For example, grasses with thin stems areof high quality.
b.
Stage of harvesting. When grasses are at the flowering stage, the quality offorage is high since nutrients are at high concentration.
c.
How well the cut pasture or fodder is dried.
d.
Length of the drying period. Prolonged drying lowers the quality of hay sincenutrients are lost through breakdown of chlorophyll and carotene. This is shownby bleaching of hay. High moisture content of hay at the time of storage canlead to fermentation and growth of mould, hence loss of nutrients.
e.
Weather conditions during harvesting and drying period. There should be no rain.
f.
Condition of the storage structure. Hay should be kept in a cool, dry, wellventilated and roofed structure.
g.
Inclusion of legume pasture such as lucerne or desmodium in the grass pasture.
Legume pastures have more protein than grass pastures.

Some of the benefits of integrated farming include:
a)
Efficient utilisation of resources and hence reduction of production costs. Forexample, livestock wastes and fish waste water can be used as manure for cropswhile crop residues and fodder crops are used as livestock feed.
b)
Soil conservation through use of organic manures and recycling of nutrientsbetween crops, livestock and fish. In addition, when trees are grown on land,
their roots hold the soil firmly thus preventing soil erosion by water.
c)
Promotion of environmental sustainability. For example, the use of chemicalfertilisers which pollute soil is reduced when organic manures generated in thefarm are used in the garden. Animal waste, crop residues and wood within thefarm can be used as sources of energy at the household level thus minimisingthe use of fossil fuels which pollute our environment.
d)
Diversification of income sources throughout the year.
e)
Promotion of food security and nutrition. Practising different farming activitiesreduce the risk of total food production failure.
f)
High yields per unit area.
Before practising integrated farming, it is important to consider various factorssuch as availability of land, labour, resources and skills to manage the variousenterprises; suitability of ecological conditions (rainfall, temperature, soil type andother environmental factors) to the proposed farming enterprises and cost of runningthe various enterprises.

In organic gardening, natural inputs such as organic manures, organic foliar feeds andnatural pesticides (bio-pesticides) are used instead of synthetic (chemical) fertilizers andpesticides. Cultural practices are used to ensure good environment for crop growth.
Examples of cultural practices include selecting pest and disease resistant crops, use ofclean seeds, crop rotation, intercropping, cover cropping, mulching, non-chemical weedcontrol, use of trap crops to control pests, proper disposal of diseased crop residuesand good sanitation in the gardens. The principal behind organic gardening is to buildhealthy soil that ensures healthy crops so that they can resist pests and diseases.
Organic gardening promotes human health and food safety and also conserves theenvironment (water, soil and animals including beneficial insects and wildlife).

The best planting site should be selected in order to grow healthy crops. The plantingsite should be fertile, well drained and free from chemical contamination. It should alsoreceive adequate sunlight. A seedbed is prepared to remove weeds and loosen the soilto ensure proper soil aeration, easy planting and water infiltration. Making natural pesticides
Natural pesticides are environmental friendly when compared to synthetic pesticides.
The aim is to repel pests so as to keep them away from the crops. We will preparesome natural pesticides (bio pesticides) using locally available materials to control pestsand some diseases in our growing crops. The best time to spray is early in the morningor the evening. This helps to avoid disintegration from the sun and to minimise harmto predators and beneficial insects such as bees
.
A few methods of preparing naturalpesticides are provided below.
Using ash brew
The alkaline nature of ash may deter some pests that attack crops. Ash from burntorganic materials such as wood or plant residues is mixed thoroughly with clean waterin a ratio of 1:10 in a clean container such as a bucket or drum. This means that forevery cup of ash, 10 cups of water are used. The mixture is left to soak for a few daysor a week depending on the type of material used to make the ash (soft or hard). Barsoap is grated into small pieces and then added into the mixture. The ratio of the soapto ash should be 1:10. The new mixture is then boiled while stirring for about 30 minutesto enhance melting of the soap. The mixture should then be allowed to cool for 1 to
2 hours. After the ash has settled, the liquid (ash brew) should be poured into anothercontainer leaving the settled ash behind. Any remaining ash particles can be filtered outusing a fine mesh strainer or cheese cloth. The natural pesticide can be sprayed on theleaves, stems and surrounding soil around the crop where pests have been noticed. Itmay be reapplied after rain or when its effectiveness decreases.

PAGE 33
Precautions
Ash containing harmful chemicals should not be used. For example, ash from plasticmaterials and wood which has been treated and painted.
Ash should not be allowed into the spray bottle or knapsack sprayer as it can clogthe nozzle.
One should wear protective gear such as gloves and safety googles when handlingthe ash or the ash brew.
Before spraying all the crops, one should spray a small area to ensure that the cropscan tolerate the ash brew.
Using hot pepper
500 grams of hot pepper fruits are crushed and then mixed with 5 litres of water. Themixture is boiled for 20 minutes and allowed to cool. After sieving the mixture, 5 litres ofwater mixed with bar soap is added to the filtrate. The pesticide can be used to controlants, aphids, flies and caterpillars.
Using garlic
10 to 12 cloves of crushed garlic are mixed with 1 litre of water. After settling for 24
hours, the mixture is strained using a piece of cloth. A cup of cooking oil is then addedto the filtrate and then sprayed on the crop. A mixture of garlic, onion and water canalso be used as a pesticide. The strong smell of the spray repels the pests away fromthe crops.
Using pyrethrum spray
20g pyrethrum powder is mixed with 10 litres water. Soap may be added to enhancelong lasting effects. The mixture is then strained and applied immediately on the pests.
In addition, dry pyrethrum flowers may be ground into powder and then dusted overinsect pests.
Using baking soda spray
Four teaspoons of baking soda are mixed in one litre of water. The mixture is added to twotablespoons of vegetable oil and two tablespoons of liquid soap and shaken vigorously.
Milk spray
One litre of milk is mixed with one litre of water and then sprayed to control mildew, afungal disease in some vegetable crops.
Using Sodom apple and Mexican marigold
Fruits of Sodom apple and Mexican marigold leaves are crushed and then soaked inwater for 2 weeks. Some liquid soap may be added to assist the mixture to stick on thecrops. The pesticide is used to repel aphids and other pests.

Field hygiene or sanitation
Seriously infected plants should be uprooted and destroyed to prevent spreading pestsand diseases. This practice is called

In addition, clean tools and equipment shouldbe used in the garden to avoid introducing diseases in the garden.
Flooding and irrigation
Flooding minimises pests which hide in the soil, for example, cutworms. Overhead irrigationcan help to dislodge aphids from the leaves and drown spider mites. However, the twomethods should target the specific pests since they can bring more problems. For example,
ooding soil can affect respiration of crop roots and also lead to soil erosion. Overheadirrigation, on the other hand, can increase chances of diseases that affect the crop leaves.
Natural predators
It is important to encourage natural predators of pests in the garden. For example,
chameleons, ladybird beetles, spiders, green lacewings, parasitic wasps and praying mantiseat various insect pests that attack crops. It is important to have a variety of plants thatprovide habitat for such beneficial organisms in an organic garden.
Crop rotation
Crops which are related should be planted in a rotation program because they areattacked by similar pests and diseases. For example, tomatoes should not be plantedin a site where potatoes, eggplant and chillies had been planted. Similarly, watermelonshould not be planted where cucumber, pumpkin or butternut had been planted in theprevious season.
Companion planting
This refers to close planting of different plants to enhance each others growth and protectthem from pests. For example, vegetables can be planted together with some herbs suchas basil, mint and rosemary which serve as pest repellents. Carrots and onions are alsogood companion crops for repelling onion flies and carrot flies respectively.

Weed control
Some weeds harbour a number of pests and disease-causing organisms (fungi, bacteriaand viruses) which can easily spread to the crops. Therefore, non-chemical methods ofweed control such as mulching, uprooting weeds and tilling land are recommended tosuppress weed growth. It is important to avoid tilling land when soil is too wet or whenit is raining because diseases can spread fast under such conditions.

Homemade sun dryer
You can determine whether the sun dryer is functioning properly by comparing theexternal temperature with the internal temperature (temperature inside the sun dryer)
after placing the thermometer on the tray for a period of time. Alternatively you canweigh the vegetables before putting them in the sun dryer and after putting theminside for sometime and compare the weights.

Using the sun dryer to dry vegetables for preservation
Once the sun dryer has been constructed and tested, it can be used to dry green vegetablesfor preservation purposes. To use the dryer:
1.
Clean the constructed dryer by wiping it with clean soapy water and rinse it bywiping with a rinsed moist piece of cloth. Remember to clean the holding trayas well.
2.
Place the sun dryer in a suitable clean environment with adequate exposure tosunlight throughout the day.
3.
Take some clean dry leafy vegetables and arrange them on the tray insidethe dryer. Ensure they are arranged in single layer and evenly spread to allowadequate air circulation.
4.
Close the sun dryers entry (door) to trap the heat inside the sun dryer.
5.
Check the drying vegetables after some few hours and turn them to ensure theydry evenly. The drying period may vary from a few days to a week dependingon the weather conditions and the amount of moisture in the vegetables.
6.
Remove the vegetables from the sun dryer once they are dried and put them ina tightly closed container for preservation.
Caution
Observe personal hygiene when handling the vegetables.
Ensure the vegetables put in the sun dryer are clean and free from contamination.
When it is very cold, especially at night, put the sun dryer inside a room to protectyour vegetables.
Take Home Activity
After learning how to construct and use a homemade sun dryer, you can make a sun dryerof a size that suits your home use. You can seek your parent,s or guardians support inacquiring available materials and constructing a sun dryer at home to be used by yourfamily. This way, you will be a responsible person who can help your family membersand friends to enhance food security at family level.
